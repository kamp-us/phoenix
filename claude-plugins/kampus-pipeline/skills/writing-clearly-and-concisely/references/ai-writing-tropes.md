The tells that mark prose as machine-generated. One instance is usually fine; the tell
is **density** — the same pattern repeated through a piece, or several patterns
clustered together. Check a draft against these, rewrite the passages that match.

## Word choice

- **Magic adverbs** — "quietly", "deeply", "fundamentally", "remarkably", "arguably"
  used to make the mundane feel significant. Cut the adverb; if it matters, the facts
  show it.
- **Overused AI vocabulary** — "delve", "leverage" (verb), "utilize", "robust",
  "streamline", "harness", "certainly". Use plain words: look at, use, strong, simplify.
- **Grandiose nouns** — "tapestry", "landscape", "paradigm", "synergy", "ecosystem" for
  anything interconnected or any field. Say what you mean.
- **The "serves as" dodge** — "serves as", "stands as", "marks", "represents" in place
  of "is". Use "is".

## Sentence structure

- **Negative parallelism** — "It's not X — it's Y" (the single most common tell), and
  the causal "not because X, but because Y". State the point directly.
- **Dramatic countdown** — "Not X. Not Y. Just Z." Lead with the point.
- **Self-posed rhetorical question** — "The result? Devastating." Merge into one
  statement, or show why.
- **Anaphora abuse** — repeating the same sentence opening ("They assume… They assume…").
  Vary openings; combine or cut.
- **Tricolon abuse** — rule-of-three back to back. Use two items or five; break the rhythm.
- **Empty transitions** — "It's worth noting", "Importantly", "Interestingly", "Notably".
  Delete and state the point.
- **Superficial "-ing" analysis** — a trailing participle that tacks shallow significance
  onto a fact ("…highlighting its importance", "…reflecting broader trends"). Give real
  analysis its own sentence, or cut.
- **False ranges** — "from X to Y" where X and Y aren't on any real scale. Just list them.
- **Gerund fragment litany** — verbless "-ing" fragments after a claim ("Fixing bugs.
  Writing features."). The claim already landed; cut them.

## Paragraph structure

- **Short punchy fragments** — one-thought sentences as standalone paragraphs for
  manufactured emphasis. Combine into real paragraphs; trust the reader.
- **Listicle in a trench coat** — a list disguised as prose with "The first… The
  second… The third…". Either write flowing prose or format it as a list.

## Tone

- **False suspense** — "Here's the kicker", "Here's the thing", "Here's where it gets
  interesting". Drop the windup.
- **Patronizing analogy** — "Think of it as…", "Imagine a world where…". Explain the
  actual thing.
- **False vulnerability** — performative "and yes, I'll admit…" candor. Show bias through
  argument, not announcement.
- **"The truth is simple"** — asserting obviousness instead of proving it. Present the
  evidence.
- **Stakes inflation** — every point reframed as world-historical. Match language to the
  actual stakes.
- **Pedagogical voice** — "Let's break this down", "Let's unpack this", "Let's dive in".
  Just present it.
- **Vague attribution** — "experts argue", "industry reports suggest" with nobody named.
  Name the source or drop it.
- **Invented concept labels** — coined "the X paradox / trap / creep" used as if
  established. Describe the phenomenon instead.

## Formatting

- **Em-dash addiction** — 20 where a human uses 2-3. Prefer commas, parentheses, or
  separate sentences.
- **Bold-first bullets** — every list item opening with a bolded lead-in. Write items as
  sentences, or use real headings.
- **Unicode decoration** — smart quotes, unicode arrows. Use straight quotes and `->`.

## Composition

- **Fractal summaries** — every section and subsection restating itself. Trust the reader;
  summarize only where the audience needs it.
- **Dead metaphor** — one metaphor beaten flat across the whole piece. Use it once or
  twice, then move on.
- **Historical analogy stacking** — rapid-fire name-drops for false authority. Pick one
  example, go deep.
- **One-point dilution** — a single thesis restated ten ways to feel comprehensive. State
  it, support it, move on.
- **Signposted conclusion** — "In conclusion", "To sum up". Just write the final thought.
- **"Despite its challenges…"** — problems raised only to be waved away toward optimism.
  Discuss them seriously or skip them.

## Quick self-check

- Same sentence structure more than twice in a row?
- "not X — it's Y", "Here's the thing", or a signposted conclusion anywhere?
- Three or more historical examples stacked back-to-back?
- Stakes inflated beyond what the content warrants?
- Would a human write a first draft this way?
